Paper Abstract: In this study, we explore the application of Large Language Models (LLMs) in \textit{Jubensha}, a Chinese detective role-playing game and a novel area in Artificial Intelligence (AI) driven gaming. We introduce the first dataset specifically for Jubensha, including character scripts and game rules, to foster AI agent development in this complex narrative environment. Our work also presents a unique multi-agent interaction framework using LLMs, allowing AI agents to autonomously engage in this game. To evaluate the gaming performance of these AI agents, we developed novel methods measuring their mastery of case information and reasoning skills. Furthermore, we incorporated the latest advancements in in-context learning to improve the agents' performance in information gathering, murderer identification, and logical reasoning. The experimental results validate the effectiveness of our proposed methods. This work aims to offer a novel perspective on understanding LLM capabilities and establish a new benchmark for evaluating large language model-based agents.

key_findings 1) Retrieval-augmented per-agent memory (MR) substantially increases agents' ability to answer factual questions about other players (Other's Q accuracy improves from ~0.305 to ~0.402). 2) Self-Refinement (SR) increases the detail/completeness of responses and further boosts factual accuracy (Other's Q avg up to ~0.471). 3) Self-Verification (SV), allowing multiple regeneration attempts and scoring for authenticity, yields additional gains (MR+SR+SV(N=3) Other's Q avg ≈ 0.498) and produces chat histories more similar to full scripts (OpenAI cosine similarity up to 0.831). 4) Agents with MR+SR+SV achieve the best downstream outcomes (higher civilian win rate and murderer identification accuracy), and more available information (e.g., Full Script Access) yields still stronger inferential performance; model capability (GPT-4 > GPT-3.5) also strongly affects reasoning performance.
challenges_or_limitations Reported issues and limitations include: (a) LLM hallucinations necessitate a verification module (SV) and the SV design still requires attempts and scoring heuristics; (b) limited context windows motivate external memory but introduce design choices (embedding model, chunking, number of retrieved items — authors used top-5); (c) variability and stochasticity of LLM outputs cause experimental variance (authors ran multiple seeds/runs but note variability remains); (d) costs and replication challenges due to proprietary model versions and compute; (e) evaluation primarily in Chinese on the Jubensha dataset, so generalization across languages/games was not established.
